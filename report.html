Introduction: What is the essential story being told by your site and what type of structure did you choose to implement?

My website is a lifestyle blog containing news for anyone interested in the alternative/goth subculture. I created this website because while I found many websites online either about horror and fantasy books and movies, or about metal music, there weren’t that many websites that would link together a diverse range of interests that a large proportion of people in this subculture find interesting. The aim of the site is to collect information about different cultural areas that are interesting to a lot of people who belong to this subculture: e.g. there are separate pages displaying horror and dark fantasy book reviews, horror movie reviews, unusual and spooky travel locations, and upcoming goth/music events. The structure is modelled on a typical review/news website where there are separate ‘listings’ pages for the different areas of culture such as movies, books or travel. These pages display a list of review titles together with clickable images; for instance, with book covers for the Book Reviews pages. Clicking on these images directs you to an article/review page. Some of the reviews pages and articles also contain and aside bar where there are links to external resources about popular gothic books, movies, or holidays. There is also a form page where you can subscribe to a newsletter.

State 3 things that have inspired you when creating your website. These could include guest speakers, other websites, artists, developers etc.

1. I was inspired by the website for Pacific Northwest Stories. This is a website for a group of horror and dark fantasy podcasts made by the Canadian artist Terry Miles. While this website is made with Squarespace, which contains ready-made templates for websites, I like the appearance and layout for the website and drew upon some of the styling ideas I found effective in it, such as the use of a dark forest background for the header, high contrast settings that make for ease of readability, and a clear division of each page into a large header, main area (with icons for the podcast listings) and footer. I also really liked how the color palette of blacks, greys and mauves reflects the mysterious and atmospheric content of the art that the website is promoting. However, the one thing I decided I would change is that the images for the icons of the podcasts on the ‘Our Shows’ page on this website take forever to load, which is really off-putting for the user. As such, I made a note to use smaller icons for the books and movies listings in my website.

2. Additionally, I was motivated to make this website because I have been attending an online horror book club for the last couple of years whichs also organizes movie events in person and in real life; I found the club on Meetup.com. I have noticed that many of the members of the book club have similar interests when it comes to not only books, but films, and music as well, and that having a website combining suggestions of these different forms of entertainment media in the goth/horror genre would be something that a lot of members would enjoy.

3. Finally, one of my inspirations was the book review website called Literary Hub What I like about this website in particular is the listing of new novels or poetry books on this page: Lithub . There are clickable images of book covers in a grid layout that looks clean and is easy to navigate. Clicking on a cover takes you to more information about that book. I liked how there is not too much information on this page, making it very user friendly: there is no lengthy description underneath the book icons, just the image and the title, which makes it relaxing to scroll through these looking for suggestions. I have also often used this site on my mobile phone as well as on a desktop computer, and I have found that the images get resized and placed into one column instead of three on smaller screens, and this responsive layout makes this a favourite website of mine to scroll through on different devices.

State 3 ways in which your site is accessible.

1. The most simple way I knew I had to make my site accessible was by including ‘alt’ HTML tags to images, labels to input fields, and aria-labels on non-text HTML elements. For example, I used an aria-label on the dropdown menu button with three bars on it, which I made using three lines as CSS divs, as this button did not have any text inside the HTML element (unlike the Load More button, which says ‘Load More…’). Therefore, a disabled user using a screenreader would not be able to know what it looks out if the screenreader was reading out the HTML, thus highlighting the importance of adding an aria-label.

2. The second way I made my site accessible to users is by implementing a Dark Mode toggle button. I personally know many people with light sensitivities chronic migraines, who dislike looking at bright lights and prefer reading with a dark mode. There is a Dark Mode button on the top-left of every page in the website, so the user does not have to search for this option in a settings menu, as it is positioned in a way to make it easily accessible.

3. Finally, I implemented different text-size options in a span of buttons for large text, medium text, small text and extra-small text, for users with visual difficulties reading small text. On the click of one of these buttons, the text of the whole page is enlarged. I did this by setting the ‘root’ font-size of the HTML document to a certain number in pixels, and then setting the font-size of all the other text elements in rem units, so that it is easily resized when the absolute root font-size changes.

State 3 ways in which you have considered usability in your site

1. I have tried to select colors for both the dark and the light modes on the site that have a high contrast ratio, so mainly black background with white text, and white background with black text. I also used red to change text color when hovering on important links or buttons, as this color is the brightest non-monochromatic color that will contrast with both black and white. For larger screens, I set the black and the white color to be slightly off from #000000 for black and #FFFFFF for white, as the contrast between pure black and pure white could seem too harsh on a large screen. However, when the screen size is reduced to a mobile-sized screen, I reset the root black and white variables to pure #000000 black and pure #FFFFFF white, as people are more likely to use a mobile screen outside in the sunlight, and therefore a higher contrast would be advantageous to the visibility in these settings.

2. I have tried to follow conventions frequently used in websites, such as placing the SPOOKTASTIC logo on the top left of the site, and the contact and copyright/date information in the footer, as these are the places users usually look for this data. I know from my own experience that when I want to reference an online resource, I always scroll to the end of the page as this is where I expect to find the date, and understand the frustration when this is in the centre of the HTML or somewhere else.

3. An important way in which I tried to make my website user-friendly was by creating a simple navigation structure. By this, I mean that for every page in the website, there need not be more than 2 clicks to take the user to any other page. For example, if I am on the page for the travel review of Washington State, USA, to get to a Book Review, I need to navigate once to go to the Book Reviews page, and once more to click on the selected book review. I think that having too many clicks to get between pages on the website makes for an exhausting user experience, which is why I tried to eliminate this problem.

State 3 things you had to learn/find out by yourself when creating your site, and describe how you did that

1. I had to work out how to store a setting on the website, so that it was applied to the whole style when the user navigated through the different pages. This was necessary for setting the text size and the Dark Mode, as I didn’t want the user to have to select the right option every time they went to a new page. I read about using the localStorage JS property to do this on here: https://blog.logrocket.com/localstorage-javascript-complete-guide/ I then had to create some functions to adapt this feature to storing the right data to keep the preferred text-size stored in the user’s browser, and decide to create a localStorage variable called ‘textSize’ and a string with ‘22px’ or ‘14px’ stored in it.

2. I had to create a Subscribe to Our Newsletter form, and I wanted to make sure it would be responsive. However, I had a problem getting the label for the text inputs to be ‘inline’ with the input boxes, so I set a responsive grid layout on the container for each label and input, which I had to play around with before it worked.

3. The most challenging thing I had to do was create an image gallery inside the articles I wrote. I wanted to increase and decrease the image size of the images in the gallery when the text size was changed, as the tiny images together with a large text size might reduce the user’s attention, as there could seem to be too much text and too few images splitting it up. Therefore, I made a global variable in globals.js to store the properties (the sizes for each text-size) of the images in the responsive gallery for each screen size, and then call a function that would change these sizes whenever the text-size was changed by the user.

What aspect(s) of your work could be improved, and how might you do things differently another time?

My main issue is that I couldn’t manage to get the aside menus to look like the links were evenly spread out with the header. I wish I had spent more time on the asides as I always find it so difficult to get them to be aligned neatly with the rest of the text. I spent a lot of time adjusting lots of CSS properties, so that the long aside on the right of the article on large screens wouldn’t look ridiculous. I also had this problem during the midterm, so I worked more on it, but still need to research more about how to make the aside element look good.

I also have a problem with the fact that when the user clicks to load/hide the reviews, or the article sections, the expansion and contraction of the content looks clunky and makes the user lose their position on the page. I would like to implement some functions that would enable the page to scroll up or down slowly when these buttons are clicked.

I also tried to integrate an automated slideshow with a user-controlled slideshow in the Seed Review HTML page, in order to adapt the slideshow code I found on the W3 schools tutorial, but somehow whenever I tried to mix these two features nothing worked. The slideshow was a bit of a last moment decision, so if I’d had more time, I definitely would have worked more on giving the slideshow both these features.

Furthermore, I wish that I had spent more time integrating video and audio into some pages about music and events, for example having an audio player that the user could control to listen to a particular song for a band being written about.
