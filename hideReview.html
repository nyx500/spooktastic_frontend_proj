A Deadly Game of Hide-and-Seek - 'Hide' by Kiersten White

Kiersten White’s first novel is a portrayal of the quintessential millennial experience masquerading as survival horror. White interweaves Greek mythology with 21st century consumerist culture to produce a fast-paced thriller with a pointed political message - due to their obsession with legacy and inheritance, previous generations have created a new form of oligarchy that has destroyed any semblance of freedom and opportunity in modern society. A system that requires the constant sacrifice of the aspirations (‘aspiring’ is used often to describe the characters in the novel) of the majority of the population, in order to maintain the security and comfort of the select few who have been fortunate enough to inherit wealth, power and connections.
Our heroine has been hiding all her life, ever since her father brutally murdered her whole family, sparing her only because she remained hidden in what was a gruesome game of hide-and-seek. Mackenzie Black has told herself that hiding is her only talent. All she wants to do is to find a flat in a dead-end town and to earn enough money to get by so that she can remain hidden forever. A solution to her perpetual homelessness appears in the form of a repetition of that gruesome childhood game – an opportunity to participate in a hide-and-seek competition, set in an abandoned amusement park, where 14 contestants have to enter the park through a mysterious gate every day from dawn until dusk. The only rule: ‘don’t be found’. The prize: $50, 000. At first, it is not apparent who has been tasked with finding the contestants – a revelation which is deliciously drawn out by White for most of the story.
The fourteen contestants initially seem like caricatures of certain millennial ‘types’, an array of creative, ambitious but undirected individuals whom society has failed. Among them we have ‘beautiful Ava’, an ‘aspiring Instagram model’, who ‘has no sponsors’, and Jaden, an ‘aspiring CrossFit gym owner’ who ‘has no gym’. Sydney is a YouTube with an unsuccessful prank show, and Christian is a, yep you guessed it, aspiring app developer – regardless of the fact that he has no idea as to which app he would like to develop. Logan is full of hope and aspiration for a better life, but with no idea or guidance in which direction he should go. Isabella, perhaps the most pathetic of all the characters, is classified as the eternal intern – she wants to meet the competition’s organizers to ‘network’ and dons a pinstriped pantsuit at every occasion. Ava, an injured veteran, and LeGrand, banished from his father’s Christian cult, are portrayed as the most sympathetic characters in the novel, and are also the ones pursuing the least attention-seeking, self-indulgent goals. Despite this somewhat large cast, White drops casual hints every now and again to remind the reader who is who, and eventually fills in the details of everyone’s backstory, which paints an expressive portrait of each individual that goes beyond the basic outline. The organizer of the hide-and-seek game is an older woman called Linda, whose behaviour creates a creeping sense of foreboding, to say the least. We receive hints from the very beginning that being ‘found’ is going to be much, much worse than just missing out on the cash prize – the masseuse treating the contestants before they enter the game is ‘crying’ as she does it, but none of them see her face.
'Hide' is an acutely perceptive satire of 21st century American capitalist society. Gary, the chef working for Linda, taunts Rebecca, an actress with nut allergies participating in the competition: “Hey-oh, look at this crew! Let me guess: You want gluten-free options. How about a cruelty-free, free-range piece of avocado toast? The avocados were raised in a hippie commune and sung to sleep every night, and they were never allowed to be friends with a single piece of gluten.”
White's thriller makes fun of the glib generalization often made by Boomers that every millennial is a sensitive “snowflake” because they all wrongly believe they’re so special – the chef asks the actress, Rebecca, if she thinks she has ‘special bowels’ because of her food allergies. As such, White creates a parody of the typical Boomer parodying what they see as the spoiled self-indulgence of the millennial generation, thus defusing its ability to oppress and humiliate. After all, whose fault is it that people like Rosiee, who wants to design silver jewellery, or Rebecca, the aspiring actress, were told they could be anything, achieve anything they wanted as long as it made them happy and fulfilled, but never told them what they should be doing?
The setting and the imagery used throughout the book also tie into the history of the American dream and the various phases of its evolution during the 20th century – in the 1940s, the founders of the park where the contest takes place sacrificed themselves to the monster dwelling within the park’s bounds to create a better society for their children, just like the Silent Generation did during the war and the years following it. Following this grim beginning, during the heyday of Western optimism and increased social mobility in the 1960s and ‘70s, the park becomes an amusement park, highlighting the pivotal role of fun and entertainment during this “Golden Age” of the American Dream. After the park closes due to a tragic incident, construction workers are brought in every few years to work on the pretext of renovation for the “park’s re-opening”, paralleling the disillusionment in progressive social change in American in the 1980s and the shift to practical Reagan and Thatcherite economics.
In 2002, the shady Keepers of the park begin organizing the Hide-and-Seek Game, staging it as a reality show – mirroring the new format of the circus for ‘the people’ in the new millennium. In each era, the way in which the monster at the heart of the park is fed becomes a distilled expression for how American capitalism works.
The founders of the park are proud of having come up with a system that guarantees harmony, wealth and abundance for their progeny – a few expendable individuals are sacrificed in the park every 7 years to an abominable and invisible Monster (a mutation of the Minotaur of antiquity) in exchange for luck and prosperity for the majority. The park’s founders who run the deadly game of hide-and-seek are proud of how they have contributed to the ‘greater good of society’ with such a low cost – their direct descendants run powerful and influential pharmaceutical and tech companies that contribute to the health of the economy, thus benefitting society as a whole. This is a blatantly barbed jibe at the beating heart of neoliberal and libertarian discourse, exposing the paradox of the ‘trolley problem’ which lies at the bottom of the distorted utilitarianism underlying the worldview of major economic decision-makers. Who today hasn’t heard neoliberal men in power making arrogant assertions about the privatization of health, culture, education and nature because it is good for the economy? This abstraction of a greedy economy that demands the sacrifice of the Hopeful and the Aspirational is akin to the Monster at the center of the amusement park that needs to be fed every 7 years. The health of the capitalist economy requires the sacrifice of the health of the unworthy. The Monster in this novel is named after the famous Minotaur in Greek mythology, Asterion – meaning star or starry. When the Monster opens its slobbering jaws to consume its victims, they see that it doesn’t have teeth – rather, its mouth opens onto a black, yawning abyss which appears to be populated by stars. When reading this, I couldn’t help but think of the truism so commonly cited in civilized, capitalist society: per ardua at astra, through adversity to the stars; a fitting motto for the cult of the American dream. However, the only stars that the contestants reach are those of death and oblivion, rather than of fulfilled aspiration.
Despite this biting social commentary, Hide is not in any sense weighed down by the dogmatic and cloying ‘wokeness’ of current attempts at social justice. Kiersten White uses humour and parody frequently to defuse any risk of heavy-handed moralizing: Atrius, a graffiti artist who ‘wants to be the next Banksy’ has misspelled his pseudonym (it was supposed to be Atreus, the name of a Greek god), making it equivalent to the name of a well-known American health-insurance company. In an attempt to save face, Atrius parries, ‘I couldn’t afford health insurance, so I decided to become insurance’. Ava, the disabled veteran, is a lesbian and a woman of color, but she is not presented as a saintlike caricature but as a fully developed individual. LeGrand, a Christian fundamentalist exiled from his father’s sect, is not demonized for being an essentially evil, demonic figure of the white Patriarchy – the misogynistic and homophobic views imparted by his religious background are explored when the author delves into his thought process, but he is not reduced to them.
The main weakness of this novel would be the occasional sense of mawkish sentimentalism that descends whenever the narrative veers towards LeGrand’s main motive – to save his gravely ill, disabled younger sister, or Mack’s guilt about her sister dying instead of her. Regrettably, White falls into the trap of extensively detailing the clichéd “I should be dead instead of her” guilt-syndrome of the survivor that lacks sufficient psychological insight and finesse, making this part of the character development seem tropey and unrealistic. I suspect most readers would see the machinations of the Amazement Park’s organizer, Linda, as sufficiently morally abhorrent without making the victims of her cruelty essentially good and self-sacrificing. Nonetheless, apart from this exception, the other descriptions of the characters’ emotions are competent and written well-enough, only occasionally slipping into generics or cliché.
Even if you are indifferent to the plight of the millennial generation in a zero-hour-contract gig economy, this book has enough tension and suspense in it to make it a decent thriller overall – White is a master at describing gruesome sounds rather than sights, which makes it all the creepier. Her use of adjectives such as wet and gurgling to describe the sounds of monsters or people dying adds a markedly visceral element to the reading experience. The Monster, invisible most of the time, is described using onomatopoeia and carefully-selected words in a way that the reader can really hear it padding about, hunting its prey. You know that things are going to end very, very badly for the characters who are ‘found’ – but this doesn’t reduce the thrill of waiting for them to be hunted.
